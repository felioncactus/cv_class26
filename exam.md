CHEAT SHEET (TERM — MEANING)
International order — pattern of interactions between political units under anarchy with shared rules, norms, and practices 
Modern international order — system based on sovereignty, global interdependence, and largely Western institutions 
Sovereignty — monopoly on the legitimate use of force within a territory 
Peace of Westphalia (1648) — event establishing the principle of state sovereignty and non-interference 
Anarchy (IR) — absence of a higher authority above states 
International system — structure of relations between states defined by power distribution (polarity) 
Polarity — distribution of power in the international system (unipolar, bipolar, multipolar) 
Unipolarity — one dominant power in the system 
Bipolarity — two dominant powers in the system 
Multipolarity — multiple major powers in the system 
Industrialization — transformation from agrarian to industrial society through technological and economic change 
Imperialism — extension of control and influence over other territories and peoples 
New Imperialism — late 19th-century competition among major powers for overseas territories 
Decolonization — process by which colonies gain independence from colonizing powers 
Realism — theory explaining international politics as competition for power under anarchy 
Classical realism — emphasizes human nature and power-seeking behavior
Structural realism — emphasizes the structure of the international system as determinant of state behavior 
Defensive realism — states seek security and avoid excessive power accumulation 
Offensive realism — states maximize power and pursue dominance 
Security dilemma — actions taken by a state to increase its security cause insecurity in other states, leading to tension and potential conflict 
Great power — a state with significant military and economic capabilities able to influence global politics
Hegemony — dominance of one state over others, especially within a region 
Balance of power — distribution of power preventing any one state from dominating
Liberalism (IR) — theory emphasizing cooperation, institutions, and interdependence 
Liberal institutionalism — institutions help states cooperate by reducing uncertainty and costs 
Democratic peace theory — democracies rarely fight each other due to norms and institutions 
Economic interdependence — mutual economic reliance that reduces incentives for conflict 
Constructivism — theory that international reality is socially constructed through ideas and identities 
Postcolonial theory — examines how colonial histories shape current global inequalities and perspectives 
Poststructuralism — critical approach analyzing how language and discourse shape power and knowledge 
Rules-based international order — system of laws, institutions, and norms created after world wars to manage relations and prevent conflict 
Interdependence — mutual reliance among states in economic and political systems
